"By strange coincidence, a thunderstorm had been brewing when Mahon, doinghis grisly work at the bungalow, was dealing with the most grisly job ofall--the head, the woman's head. He had severed it from the trunk, built ahuge fire in the sitting room, placed her head upon it, then (I owe a debthere to Edgar Wallace, who edited the transcript of the Mahon trial), then thestorm broke with a violent flash of lightning and an appalling crash ofthunder. As the head of Emily Kaye lay upon the coals, the dead eyesopened, and Mahon fled out to the deserted shore. When he nerved himselfto return, the fire had done its work. The head was never found..."
